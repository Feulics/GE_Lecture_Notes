Lecture 6 & 7: Principles of timing systems and application in Earth observation system

Time Systems

  • Dynamic Time System

    • Based on orbital motion of celestial bodies in solar system
    • Useful for describing satellite motion

  • Atomic Time System

    • Based on processes in nuclear physics
    • Useful for positioning, relativity, astronomy, etc.

  • Civilian Time System

    • Namely: Julian Date
    • Based on number of days elapsed since Jan 1 4,713 BC.
    • Useful for scientific computations
    • UTC for connection of all 3 time systems

  • Natural Time System

    • Namely: Sidereal Time and Universal Time

      • Defined based on diurnal rotation of Earth

    • From Earth motion to atoms oscillation

    • useful for Earth-based observations in the CTRFs

    • for transferring between CCRF to CTRFs

Requirement for Time System Definition

  1. Periodic Event
    • Rotation of Earth, orbital motion of Earth around sun, orbital motion of moons around Earth, Pendulum motion, motion of atoms, etc.
  2. Counter
  3. Reference Epoch and Time Scale
    • Amount of events within specific interval

Relationship between atomic scale and dynamic time scale

Definition of Second

  • Until 50s: Second based on length of mean solar day

    • Mean solar day = 24h*3600=86400s
    • 1s = 1/86400 mean solar day

  • But: Earth's rotation not uniform enough to measure second: Short-term & Long-term irregularity

  • -> Redefine second on orbital (Newtonian) motion of Earth around sun

    • Solar (tropical) year (Based on sun's ephemeris)
    • Ephemeris second (adopted 1952) as fraction of length of solar day at 1900

  • Not considered: Relativistic Theory & Effects of planet etc. on Earth rotation around sun

  • -> Dynamic time scale

Sidereal Time

  • Definition: hour angle of vernal equinox

    • Defined in quasi inertial CCRS
    • reflects actual rotation of Earth & precession and nutation effects of equinox

  • Difference between Apparent ST (AST) and Mean ST (MST) due to nutation

  • Scale in ST system: mean sidereal day

    • 2 consecutive transits of the mean vernal point accross the same meridian (corrected for polar motion)
      • 1 sid. day = 24 sid. hrs = 86400 sid. sec

  • Relationship between AST & MST alias Equation of the Equinox

    • AST = MST+\Delta\Psi \cos \varepsilonAST=MST+ΔΨcos⁡εAST = MST+\Delta\Psi \cos \varepsilonAST=MST+ΔΨcosε
      • \Delta\PsiΔΨ\Delta\PsiΔΨ is nutation component in longitude
      • \varepsilonε\varepsilonε is inclination angle

  • Attention: Precession: Mean Vernal Point move w.r.t true vernal point

    • m = 4612.4362 (arcSec/cent.) + 2.79312 (arcSec/cent^2)T

      • T is Julian centuries

    • 24 hours of mean ST less than one complete rotation

      • ST day 0.0084sec shorter

  • Remember: GAST for transformation from CCRS to mean CTRS

Universal Time UT

  • Definition: Hour Angle of Sun

    • In CTRS
    • Actual rotation of Earth and effects of polar motion

  • No Precession error, like vernal point

  • Fictious mean sun moving on equator with constant velocity

  • Scale

  • : Mean Solar day: 2 consecutive transits of the mean sun across same meridian (corrected for polar motion)

  • 1 Sol. day = 24 Sol. hrs = 86400 Sol. sec

Irregularity in UT

  • Irregularity due to Earth's rotation in CCTS

  • UT from observation at station

    • local meridian w.r.t instantaneous pole
    • rotation axis varies due to polar motion

  • UT0: UT from observations w.r.t true (instantaneous) pole position

    • contains variation in rotation due to polar motion

  • UT1: determined w.r.t CTP

    • corrected polar motion

  • UT1 = UT0+\Delta\lambda_{pole}UT1=UT0+ΔλpoleUT1 = UT0+\Delta\lambda_{pole}UT1=UT0+Δλpole​

  • UT1 still experiences small irregularities

Julian Date

  • In Scientific work: Continuous counting/dating more convenient

    • Civil Calendar has months and years of varying length

  • 36 525 days per century

  • Fundamental Epoch: 12h UT on 01.01.4713 B.C.

    • sufficiently preceding historical record

  • Julian day number as day in continuous count since fundamental epoch

  • JD of standard epoch of UT: J2000.0

    • 2 451 545.0JD = 12H UT on 01.01.2000

  • For convenience: Modified Julian Date (MJD):

    • MJD = JD - 2,400,000.5
    • Subtracted half a day, to start at midnight

Atomic Time

  • First realised in 1955

  • Origin for atomic time officially: UT1 time 0h 0m 0s (midnight) 01.01.1958

  • International Atomic Time (TAI) officially adopted January 1972

  • TAI realised over 200 high-precision atomic clocks around the world located at sea level

  • Worldwide synchronisation about 100nanoSec

  • Alias: Statistical clock/time

    • TAI computed (like weighted average) from many atomic clocks

  • All civil clocks set w.r.t. a TAI standard

Concept

  • Scale scaled to the duration of the ephemeris second

  • Precision: Consider General Relativistic effects from spatially varying gravitational potential

    • -> defined for rotating geoid (mean sea level) in Earth frame not in barycentric system

  • Atomic Second: Duration of 9 192 631 700 period of the radiation

    • Corresponding to transition between 2 hyperfine levels of ground state of Cesium 133 atom

    • e^-e−e^-e− close to nucleus have higher energy

    • Known energy gap between low and high energy

    • Apply exactly the specific amount of energy via microwaves (9.192631700 MHz9.192631700MHz9.192631700 MHz9.192631700MHz)

Estimate LoD with satellite orbital analysis

  • Determined from orbital analysis for each satellite mission

Changes in LoD

  • Long-term trend

    • Caused by:
      • Core-Mantle friction
      • Tidal friction
      • Glacial Rebound

  • Inter-decadal variations (~8.6 years)

    • Changes due to Geomagnetic jerks
      • sudden changes in the Earth's geomagnetic field observed every 3-10 years

  • Seasonal variation

  • Effect of human

    • Pumping of 2.150 gigatons of groundwater: 6mm sea level rise from 1993-2010
    • Redistribution of water from equatorial regions to poles:
      • Accelerates Earth's rotation
